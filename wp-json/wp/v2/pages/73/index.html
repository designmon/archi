[ps2id id=’agropolis’ target=”\/] Hanging Gardens of Agropolis
<\/strong><\/span>Master’s thesis | at CoLab | Prof. Ignacio Borrego | 2019\/20<\/span>
Sustainable urban food production intertwined with public spaces of leisure on hilltops of Athens<\/span><\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

Hanging Gardens of Agropolis is a food production facility intertwined with public spaces on the Philopappos Hill across the Acropolis, which is to become the forerunner of urban farming movement in Athens and expand on further hilltops of the city.<\/p>\r\n

 <\/p>\r\n



<\/p>\r\n

The project respects the existing nature and creates a new elevated layer over the park by taking the smallest possible footprint and incorporating the street as a part of the building.\u00a0

<\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

The majority of the site becomes a food forest which produces a highly diversified harvest on a relatively small area. The fields are organized in terraces following the topography and providing horizontal connections across the site and occasional niches for relaxation, beekeeping or individual garden cultivation.\u00a0<\/p>\r\n

Ground floor<\/p>\r\n

Functional cores are placed parallel to the streets and create base for elevated volumes and a framework for flexible use. Thus the street can become a weekly market, wedding venue, concert site or simply a shaded public space outside.<\/p>\r\n

 <\/p>\r\n

First floor<\/p>\r\n

Second floor<\/p>\r\n

 <\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n


The program is divided based on natural law of Archimedes, which makes the hot air rise and cold air drop. The vertical geography of the heat creates multiple climate zones within one structure. Thus, one of the volumes accommodates an aquaponic greenhouse with an intertwined level for SPA, the other one – production facility with a level for community center.<\/p>\r\n

 <\/p>\r\n

Section AA<\/p>\r\n

 <\/p>\r\n

Section BB<\/p>\r\n

The juxtaposition is planned in a way that does not hinder the production, but allows for a one of a kind thermal and visual experience which leads to raising awareness about food production and environment. The project has a potential to partly relieve the overcrowded Acropolis Hill and creates value for the locals in the strongly tourist-oriented area.<\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

The sloped roofs resemble those of a typical greenhouse and allow for rain water collection for watering the fields and for the building facilities. The roof is sloped at 29o <\/sup>angle optimized for installation of photovoltaic pannels. The north-facing sides of the roof are glazed getting ambient daylight into the building. Mechanical openings in the\u00a0 roof allow for natural ventilation and temperature regulation. Further, geothermal energy is sourced and exploited through the buildings direct uses: heating the greenhouse, food processing such as crop drying.<\/p>\r\n

[ps2id id=’pm25′ target=”\/]PM 2.5
<\/span><\/strong>with Olga Bialczak, Katharina Kocol
<\/span>at CHORA: Conscious City | Prof. Raoul Bunschotten | 2016\/17<\/span>
<\/span><\/strong>Real-time responding, sound and air pollution absorbing facade system.\u00a0\u00a0<\/span><\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

Equipped in sound sensors, PureMatter 2.5 responds in real-time to current environmental conditions adjusting the size of its opening when the noise exceeds the non-harmful level (80db). \u00a0<\/span><\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

The modules are covered in a naturally abundant raw material, sodium alginate (a polysaccharide that can be extracted from seaweed and algae) synthesized with a high-volume industrial by-product, silica fume (a by-product of ferrosilicon or silicon metal alloy processing) – a \u201cgreen absorbant\u201d that captures particulate matter. The movable modules, made of perforated aluminium panels with PET sound absorbing core, reduce the sound levels up to 40db (quiet library sounds). The size of the opening can be manually adjusted by the user & be additionaly used as an integrated shading system during summer months. \u00a0 Producing the modules in S, M & L sizes allows its application on different scales and typologies: from stadiums\u2019 facades to prevent spreading the noise, to residential buildings – to provide a good night\u2019s sleep. \u00a0<\/p>\r\n



\u00a0 <\/p>\r\n

 <\/p>\r\n

The 1:1 concept prototype puts theory in practice. Built using generally available parts, equipped with sound sensors and programmed with Arduino, it reflects the way the single facade module works. \u00a0<\/p>\r\n

[ps2id id=’cyclops’ target=”\/] Cyclops
<\/strong><\/span>with Katharina Kocol, Undine Kimmel
at AA Visiting School – Animalesque | Prof. Raoul Bunschotten | 2018<\/span>
Prototype of a cohabitation hive for urban wildlife, aimed at starting a social movement to protect the ecosystem in Berlin. \u00a0<\/span><\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

Inspired by nature\u2019s shapes and behaviors, Cyclops is made of rope, which creates a flexible and expandable system. To protect the interior from the rain, the thinner filling structure is impregnated with beeswax. Thus, the prototype retains a raw character that is reminiscent of a natural hive.<\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

 <\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

 <\/p>\r\n

Based on the Macram\u00e9 square knot, the branched system design can result in different and often surprising structures that deliver a variety of incubators applicable on all urban scales. As a result, it helps to raise common awareness about bees among urban wildlife and promotes the cultivation of green environments within big cities.<\/p>\r\n

 <\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

 <\/p>\r\n

In the summer of 2017, a 1:1 prototype was hung on a tree in the Aedes garden in Berlin. Once inhabited by Apis Mellifera bees, Cyclops becomes the Mother-Hive that embodies the complexity of the bee organism, with its branching system spreading the cohabitants across the city.<\/p>\r\n

[ps2id id=’intertwined’ target=”\/] Intertwined
<\/strong><\/span>with Katharina Kocol
at Political Spaces: Urban Frictions | Prof. Charlotte Malterre-Barthes | 2018\/19<\/span>
Residential project with a community center as an alternative to a luxury residential development | Place of dignity and equal chances \u00a0<\/span><\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

Located in Berlin\u2019s red-light district, the site in Kurf\u00fcrstenstra\u00dfe, Berlin is a hotspotfor prostitution and the consumption of illegal narcotics. Despite the area\u2019s central location,its reputation hasled to cheaper rent levels, thus attracting renters such as artists, students and the less well-off. Theproject currently being built there – Schoenegarten, is potentially the result of misplaced capital and could soon become a ghost building.<\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

Intertwined offers a trade-off that reacts to the friction in the local community. It offers a viable alternative strategy for investors, who would benefit from an improved floor area ratio(FAR) in exchange for ceding a portion for the public good.<\/p>\r\n

Second floor \u00a0<\/p>\r\n

Section \u00a0<\/p>\r\n

The residential offering is enriched by a community center, creating a place of dignity and equality for members of the public, regardless of social station. The community center is accessed through separate translucent marble towers – the jewels of the building – located next to the apartments. Intertwined introduces an element of heterogeneity that moderates rapid gentrification and segregation, honoring our social obligation to support marginalized groups of people.<\/p>\r\n

[ps2id id=’lightpath’ target=”\/] Light path
<\/strong><\/span>with Daniel Dieren, Esraa Refat, Katia Schmit
at Natural Building Lab | Prof. Eike Roswag | 2019<\/span>
Carbon-neutral museum for a middle-age settlement D\u00fcppel created collaborating with the local community.<\/span> \u00a0<\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

Inspired by the vernacular construction of D\u00fcppel\u2019s site, the museum represents the fluidity of forests and serene atmospheres. The thick curved cob walls hold a light multiaxial timber roof structure with multiple skylights and trees growing through, filtering the light and thereby creating a forest-like atmosphere. \u00a0<\/p>\r\n

\u00a0 <\/p>\r\n

Rethinking the construction standard, the building is no longer seen as an insulated envelope. Clay provides good insulation, without additional protective layers. The central exhibition space, which is part of the museum, adapts to ever-changing daily weather conditions. \u00a0<\/p>\r\n

[ps2id id=’hyperkatoikia’ target=”\/]Hyperkatoikia
<\/strong><\/span>with Kassandra Sarantis
at LIA: Laboratory for integrative architecture | Prof. Finn Geipel | 2017<\/span>
High-rise project of 130 social housing apartments with the possibility of individual expansion\u00a0
<\/span><\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

The project is to be developed and operated as an experimental prototype by a Berlin housing association. The project draws inspiration from the Greek typology of Polykatoikia (Greek for \u201cmultiple\u201d and \u201capartments\u201d, or multi-residence) is based on the Dom-Ino construction system (open floor plan modular structure) that significantly alleviated housing shortages in Athens during the post-war era. \u00a0

<\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

Sixteenth floor

<\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

Seventeenth floor

<\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

Modules<\/p>\r\n

Each Hyperkatoikia housing unit comes with an additional \u201cempty space\u201d that can be transformed with relative ease. The flexibility gives low-income communities an opportunity to improve their living standard by increasing the existing amount of productive space, which can be rented out or used for self-employment. The initial building provides a supportive framework to facilitate the expansion process so that all apartments can be connected and adapted to the varying needs of the residents.

<\/p>\r\n

<\/p>\r\n

Despite living in the middle of the bustling city, each inhabitant can contribute to the design of the building\u2019s exterior. As such, the inhabitants get a degree of freedom over customization, a privilege typically only reserved for single-family house owners. Consequently, the inhabitants are more apt to develop a sense of belonging, creating a safer and more inclusive neighborhood.

[ps2id id=’about’ target=”\/] \u00a0About<\/strong><\/p>\r\n

Hello!<\/p>\r\n

My name is Monika and I have gained over year of experience working in an interdisciplinary urban research lab and architectural offices.<\/p>\r\n

Statement<\/strong><\/span><\/p>\r\n\r\n

Architecture is a collaborative practice. Great projects come to life through the joint efforts of an inspired team that feels empowered to touch the lives of people in a way that spans multiple creative disciplines. Designing for a community, acknowledging diverse perspectives and honoring vulnerabilities are collaborative processes that transcend disciplinary boundaries and unite us in a common vision to shape the world we live in.<\/p>\r\n

As the framework of our everyday lives, architecture serves as the center of gravity for many social and environmental issues. To respond to rapid changes caused by digitalization, there must be open dialogue about income inequality, pollution, climate change, social vulnerability, urban congestion and the scarcity of public spaces.<\/p>\r\n

Nonetheless, dialogue is only the first step; we must take action to enact real change. Modern problems require modern solutions. Many of these solutions can be found at the intersection of architecture and other disciplines such as STEM, social sciences, public policy and design. Hence, every human being, regardless of professional or academic background,has something to contribute. Through experimentation, prototyping of ideas and iterative testing, we can discover solutions that audaciously challenge the status quo. In so doing, we make the complex approachable, the imaginary real, the invisible visible and the unheard heard, for the benefit of all.
